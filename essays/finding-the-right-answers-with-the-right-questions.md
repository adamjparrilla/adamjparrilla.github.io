## How To Ask Questions The Smart Way

To get smart answers, you first need to know how to properly ask smart questions. Before reading How To Ask Questions The Smart Way by Eric Steven Raymond and Rick Moen, I didn’t know very much about the interaction in the open-source community. This article illustrated how to properly interact online so that you can effectively produce answers for yourself, others and avoid wasting time.

## My Main Takeways

One main takeaway that I enjoyed spoke on what to do before asking your question, like taking a moment to look for yourself. I’m not going to pretend like I’ve never done this, I’ve often panicked when needing answers and jumped straight to asking teachers or TA’s. But looking back now, I bet that instead, I could have simply found it by searching the web, reading the manual, or checking with a friend. In doing this you put yourself in the best position to solve your problem and if nothing comes out of it, at least it shows the effort put forth by yourself, motivating someone to take out time to help you.
Another great idea that I’ve learned from How To Ask Questions The Smart Way, is how to be precise in asking a question. This first starts with where you’re posting your question online, for example, you want to post it in a relevant forum so that it’s on topic. Being precise also means knowing how to describe your issue so that another person can help. For instance, describing the environment the problem occurs in is one way, the research you’ve already done, or any other possible relevant changes you’ve made are just a few ways mentioned to get the response you need.

## Question Examples

After looking online at StackOverflow, a professional online question and answer site for programmers, one example that I found was a great question. Here, this person was trying to create a custom exception for their program but found trouble when they try to extend the exception class. Here they do a great job describing exactly what they did, while also talking about what they’ve done to troubleshoot so far. They even provide a link to a video they’ve watched to try to solve this issue as well as the code causing the error. This person has thought through this issue and that led to someone giving a direct answer so that they could solve it. (click [here](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MCXpzEodSf0). to visit this question)
A final example I’ve found on StackOverflow shows what you shouldn’t do when asking a question. Here this person asked “When using the SAVEPOINT and ROLLBACK, what will be the output of this?” (StackOverflow). The main problem with this question is the lack of information provided, as all this person has given, other than the question is code with nothing to explain what is happening. Not only does this mean their answers will be bad, but it also just makes it so no one wants to respond to you.

## Overall Thoughts

In conclusion, learning to ask questions the smart way will greatly benefit not only my programming but can easily be taken into everything you do. Don’t just blindly ask and hope for good responses, actually learn about what you’re doing and you’ll gain more knowledge because you’re putting yourself in the position to get it.
